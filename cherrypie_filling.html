Crust and Crumble: A Cherry's Tale

Cherry pie is a slice of Americana with roots that stretch far beyond the Fourth of July. Cherries themselves have been around for millennia, first cultivated in the regions around the Black Sea and Caspian Sea. The Romans were big fans, spreading cherry trees across Europe as they expanded their empire. By the time cherries made their way to North America with English colonists in the 1600s, they were already steeped in centuries of sweet and sour tradition.

But cherry pie is a story of settlers, seasons, and sugar: early American cooks leaned into pies as a practical way to preserve fruit and stretch ingredients. Cherries, with their bold flavor and short harvest window, were a natural fit into that lifestyle. Toss them with a little sugar, tuck them into a flaky crust, and you had a treat that could brighten even the bleakest winter day.

This sweet/tart pie really hit its stride in the 1800s, when sour cherries (especially the Montmorency variety) became the go-to for baking. Their tartness balanced beautifully with sugar, and they held their shape like champs in the oven. Canning only made things better. Suddenly, you didn‚Äôt have to wait for cherry season. You could pop open a jar of ruby-red filling and bring summer to the table any time of year.

Cherry pie has been tied to everything from George Washington‚Äôs mythical cherry tree to diner counters and state fairs. It‚Äôs a symbol of nostalgia, of home, of something lovingly made by hand. Here's a story you can serve warm, with a scoop of vanilla ice cream and a whole lot of heart the next time you whip up some cherry pie filling.
