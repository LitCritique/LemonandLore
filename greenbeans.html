Lore from the Beanstalk

Green beans may not be flashy, but they’ve been quietly feeding the world for thousands of years. These slender pods, also known as snap beans or string beans, have roots in Central and South America, where Indigenous peoples were cultivating them alongside corn and squash in the legendary “Three Sisters” planting method. Together, these crops supported each other in the soil and on the plate, forming the backbone of ancient agricultural wisdom.

When Spanish and Portuguese explorers arrived in the Americas, they brought green bean seeds back to Europe, where the beans quickly took root in gardens and kitchens. By the 1600s, green beans were a staple across continents, prized for their tender texture and quick cooking time. Originally, green beans had a tough, fibrous “string” running down the seam—hence the name “string beans.” It wasn’t until the late 19th century that plant breeders developed stringless varieties, making them easier to prep and more popular than ever.

In the canning world, green beans are a classic. Whether pressure-canned for pantry storage or pickled with garlic and dill for a tangy snack, they’re a favorite for their versatility and long shelf life. During wartime rationing and the rise of Victory Gardens, canned green beans became a symbol of self-reliance and homegrown resilience.

Today, they’re still a garden favorite because they're easy to grow, quick to harvest, and endlessly adaptable. From casseroles to crisp dilly beans, green beans carry a legacy of nourishment, tradition, and quiet strength.

Green Beans-Canning-Raw Pack

📅 First Made: June 2024 🔄 Last Made: June 2024 📝 Recipe Version: 1.0

What I Learned

  • 💡 Learning point 1: Using the raw pack method means you don’t have to pre-cook your green beans before canning. It’s a huge time-saver during peak harvest season when you’re working through baskets of beans.
  • 💡Learning point 2: Green beans shrink during processing, so packing them tightly into jars (without crushing them) helps prevent floating beans and ensures a better fill.
  • 💡Learning point 3:Adding salt to each jar is for flavor, not preservation. You can skip it if you’re watching sodium or want more flexibility when using the beans later.

Tips from Experience

Pro Tip: Trim beans to fit your jars vertically. This is not just for aesthetics! Standing them up helps you pack more in, reduces floating, and makes for a beautiful presentation when you pop open a jar mid-winter.

Green Beans-Canning-Raw Pack

⏱️ Prep Time: 30 mins
🔥 Processing Time: 25 mins
🏺 Yield: 4 quarts
📅 Season: Summer

Equipment Needed

  • Pressure canner
  • 6–7 quart jars or 8–10 pint jars with new lids and rings
  • Large pot (for boiling water)
  • Knife and cutting board
  • Jar lifter and funnel
  • 1 clean cloth

Ingredients

  • Fresh green beans (about 2 lbs per quart jar)
  • Boiling water
  • Canning salt: 1 tsp per quart ½ tsp per pint

Instructions

🥄 Prep Steps:

  1. Prep jars and canner: Wash jars, lids, and rings in hot, soapy water. Rinse well. Keep jars hot until ready to use. Prepare your pressure canner according to manufacturer instructions.
  2. Prep green beans: Wash beans thoroughly. Drain. Remove strings and trim ends. Cut into 2-inch pieces.

    3. 🫙 Jar Filling:

  3. Pack jars: Pack raw green beans tightly into hot jars, leaving 1 inch headspace.
  4. Add salt (optional): Add 1 tsp canning salt per quart jar (½ tsp per pint).
  5. Add boiling water: Ladle boiling water over beans, maintaining 1 inch headspace.
  6. Seal jars: Wipe rims clean with a damp towel. Place lids on jars and screw bands on until fingertip-tight.

    7. 🔥 Pressure Canning:

  7. Process your jars: Place jars in the pressure canner. Quarts: Process for 25 minutes at 10 lbs pressure Pints: Process for 20 minutes at 10 lbs pressure
  8. Cool and store:Allow canner to depressurize naturally. Remove jars and place on a towel-lined surface. Cool undisturbed for 12–24 hours.
  9. Check seals:Press the center of each lid. Lids should not flex when pressed. Label and store in a cool, dark place for up to 1 year.
    10. Refrigerate any unsealed jars and use within 2 weeks.

Notes

  • Use Fresh, Crisp Beans: For best texture and flavor, use freshly picked green beans. Older beans may turn mushy after canning.
  • Pressure Canning Only: Green beans are a low-acid food and must be pressure canned for safety. Do not attempt to water bath can them.
