Cranberry Chronicles

Jellied cranberry sauce has earned its spot as a holiday icon, and not just because it holds the shape of the can like a champ. This wobbly wonder has a surprisingly rich backstory that starts way before it hit the Thanksgiving table.

Cranberries are native to North America, and Indigenous peoples were the first to use them for food, medicine, and dyes. These bitter berries were a way of life for many tribes in the northeaster and Great Lakes regions. Some of the key tribes known for using cranberries are:

  • Algonquin
  • Wampanoag
  • Lenni-Lenape
  • Ojibwe (Chippewa)
  • Cree
  • Iroquois
Fast-forward to the 1800s, and cranberry farming was booming in the Northeast. But the berries had a short shelf life (just a few weeks in the fall) so growers needed a way to stretch the season.

Enter Marcus Urann, a lawyer-turned-cranberry farmer with a knack for innovation. In 1912, he started canning cranberry sauce to make it available year-round. His creation? Ingredients

  • 16 cups cranberries (fresh or frozen)
  • 4 cups apple juice
  • 5 cups sugar
  • 2 cups water
  • orange zest from 2 oranges (optional)

Instructions

  1. Prepare: canner, jars, and lids.
  2. Combine: cranberries, apple juice, and water in a large stainless steel saucepan. Bring to a boil. Reduce heat and keep at a simmer for about 10 minutes until cranberries soften and skins burst. Remove from heat and let cool for 5 minutes.
  3. Transfer: the cranberry mixture to a food mill or saucemaker to remove skins. Alternatively, blend in a blender until completely smooth.
  4. Return: cranberry puree to the saucepan. Add sugar and bring to a boil, stirring constantly. Once the sugar is dissolved, and the mixture keeps boiling even when stirred, set the timer for 10 minutes. Stir constantly. At ten minutes, remove from heat and test to see if the gel stage has been reached. If not, keep boiling for another 5 minutes and test again. If the gel stage has been reached, stir in orange zest..

  6. Place: jars in the canner. Water should cover the jars by at least one inch.
