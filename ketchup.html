Lore of Ketchup

Ketchup‚Äôs story is way older, and way weirder, than most people realize. Long before it became the go-to burger buddy, it started out as a fermented fish sauce in ancient China. oh yeah...you read that right. The original k√¥e-chiap was a briny blend of pickled fish and spices, popular around 300 BC and valued for its bold flavor and long shelf life. The word ‚Äúketchup‚Äù likely comes from the Hokkien Chinese term for ‚Äúfish sauce,‚Äù though some say it‚Äôs rooted in Malay or even Arabic influences.

British traders got a taste of it in Southeast Asia in the 1600s and brought it home, where they, of course, promptly reinvented it. By the 1700s, ketchup recipes in England featured everything from mushrooms and walnuts to oysters and plums. The trusty tomato wasn't even on the radar yet!

That all changed in 1812 though, when American scientist, James Mease, published the first tomato-based ketchup recipe, calling tomatoes ‚Äúlove apples‚Äù for their supposed aphrodisiac powers. But early tomato ketchup spoiled quickly, so producers added preservatives like coal tar and sodium benzoate. There was a brief moment in the 1830s when ketchup was sold as medicine by Dr. John Cook Bennett who claimed it could cure indigestion and turned it into tomato pills. Finally, in 1876, our own American entrepreneur, Henry Heinz, stepped in with a cleaner, safer version. His glass-bottled ketchup was a game-changer, so he co-founded the HJ Heinz Company of Pitssburgh, Pennsylvania and his ketchup is still the gold standard to this day.

Today, ketchup is a global food staple, found in 97% of American homes thanks to its sweet and tangy taste and its endlessly adaptable uses.

Equipment Needed

  • 1 food mill or sauce maker
  • Cheesecloth
  • 1 blender
  • 1 water-bath canning pot
  • Jar lifter and funnel

Ingredients

  • 2 teaspoons celery seeds
  • 4 individual whole cloves (no teaspoons or tablespoons--just count them out)
  • 5 individual whole allspice
  • 3 cups vinegar half white and half cider (5% acidity)
  • 24 lbs tomatoes
  • 23 cups chopped onions
  • 1/2 teaspoon black pepper
  • 1/2 teaspoon cayenne pepper
  • 2 cups granulated sugar
  • 1/4 cup canning or pickling salt
  • 1 teaspoon dried garlic

Instructions

  1. Preheat oven: Turn oven to 425¬∫F
  2. Make a spice bag: Take cheesecloth and cutting out a square. Add celery seeds, cloves, and all spice on top of the cheese cloth, then tie in a knot or use twine to tie it together. (Some of the celery seeds may fall out, that‚Äôs ok!)
  3. In a stainless steel saucepan, add the vinegar and the spice bag. Bring to a boil over high heat, then remove from heat and let it sit for a half hour with the spice bag in it. Then discard the spice bag.
  4. Meanwhile, cut tomatoes in half and core. (For Roma or San Marzano tomatoes, no need to core, just cut in half.) Place tomatoes skin down, with one layer on baking trays and roast tomatoes for 1 hour at 425¬∫F.
  5. While the tomatoes cook, saut√© the onions for 5-10 minutes on medium low heat until soft and translucent.
  6. After the tomatoes finish, use a food mill or sauce maker to remove skins and seeds. *Include all the released tomato juices on the tray as well as roasted tomatoes to food mill ensure proper acidity. Discard the seeds and skins.
  7. Place tomato sauce in a very large stainless steel saucepan. Add onions, sugar, salt, vinegar, cayenne pepper, black pepper, and dried garlic to the saucepan with the tomatoes.
  8. Bring to a boil, then reduce the heat and let it simmer, stirring frequently, until it becomes the desired thickness.
  9. While reducing the liquid, prepare a water bath canner and half pint jars.
  10. Working in small batches, transfer the mixture to the blender and puree until very smooth. Return all the pureed ketchup to the pot.
  11. Ladle hot ketchup into half pint jars leaving a 1/2 inch of head space. Wipe rim. Center lid on the jar. Screw on the band with until it is finger tip-tight.
  12. Place jars in canner and ensure the jars have at least one inch of water above the tops of the jars.
    13. Working in small batches, transfer the mixture to the blender and puree until very smooth. Return all the pureed ketchup to the pot.
  13. Bring to a boil and process for 15 minutes. Remove lid, turn off heat, wait 5-10 minutes then remove jars. Let cool and store.

Notes

  • Do not change the ratios in the recipe. It has a specific amount of acid to keep it safe for water bath canning.
  • If you end up with slightly more or less yield, don't worry. Tomatoes vary in water content and this can lead to the yield being a little different.
