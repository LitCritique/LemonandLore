Beetlore

Beets have been staining fingers and winning hearts for thousands of years. Native to the Mediterranean, early beets were lauded more for their leafy tops than their roots. It wasn‚Äôt until Roman times that the bulbous root we know today started showing up on dinner tables, and even then, it was often used medicinally rather than culinarily.

As many of these recipes do, skip up the timeline to the 19th century, and beets found their sweet spot...literally. Sugar beets became a major source of refined sugar in Europe, especially during wartime shortages. Meanwhile, the deep red garden beet became a staple in Eastern European kitchens, starring in dishes like borscht and pickled salads.

Spiced beets, with their warm notes of clove, cinnamon, and vinegar, are a uniquely American twist. Popularized in the early 20th century, they became a pantry favorite for their long shelf life and vibrant flavor. Home canners embraced them for their beauty in the jar and their versatility on the plate, ranging from holiday spreads to everyday salads.

Beets are more than just pretty roots. They‚Äôre packed with folate, fiber, and antioxidants like betalains, which give them their signature hue. In folklore, they‚Äôve symbolized love, vitality, and even protection. Some cultures believed hanging a beet in your home could ward off evil spirits. So whether you‚Äôre canning them for winter or slicing them into a summer salad, know that you‚Äôre part of a long, colorful tradition that might just keep the evil away, if only until the red washes off your skin.

Tips from Experience

Pro Tip: Be prepared for everything, and I do mean everything, in your working area, including you, to be red from all the juice. Make a paste by mixing baking soda with a little water, rub the paste onto the stained area of your hands, rinse, and then wash with soap and water. You can use this paste on anything that is splattered with the juice, but work fast on countertops as that color will set in quick!
