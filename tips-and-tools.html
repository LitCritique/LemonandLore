Tips & Tools for Happy Canning

Note: I don't use affiliate links, and I'm not being paid to recommend any products or websites. Any links included are simply there for your convenience to help you find the tools and supplies I mention more easily.

When it comes to the tools of the trade, not every item on the shelf is worth your money or time. I like to categorize gear into three basic categories: must-haves, nice-to-haves, and luxury items.
  • Must-haves are the essentials. These are non-negotiable if you want to can safely and avoid spoilage.
  • Nice-to-haves make the process easier but aren’t strictly necessary. Think of these as time-savers and mess-reducers.
  • Luxury items are the splurges. They’re great if you’re canning often or in large quantities, but you don’t need them to get started.
Every canner has their own preferences when it comes to tools and materials, so no two “must-have” lists will look exactly the same. That’s part of the fun: what works for one canner may not work for another. The key is to start with the basics. Once you get a feel for what works best for you, you can always add or upgrade as you go.

Must Haves:

    Several items are already available in the standard kitchen:
    • Large Pot: For boiling water bath canning or for heating ingredients like brines.
    • Smaller pots: For heating lids and other tasks.
    • Measuring cups and spoons: For accurate ingredient measurements.
    • Knives: For food preparation, especially paring and chef's knives.
    • Tongs: For safely handling hot jars and ingredients.
    Other items may need to be purchased:
  • Canning Funnels: Helps to fill jars neatly without spills while also maintain sterility of the working area.
  • Jar Lifter: Essential for safely lifting hot jars in and out of the canner.
  • Headspace Measuring Tool: Helps ensure proper headspace in the jars.
  • Stockpot: For water bath canning.
  • Jar Rack that matches your Stockpot: For keeping the glass jars off the bottom of the pot so water can circulate.
  • Mason Jars: Mason Jars come in all different sizes. The sized used most often in canning are half- pint, pint, and quart size. Pint and quart size jars can come in either regular mouth size or wide mouth size. I highly suggest just trying to find some locally because they are glass don’t ship easily. Plus they are way cheaper in a local store.
  • Canning Jar Rings and Lids: In most cases, you won’t need to buy these separately. If you buy jars, they will come with lids and rings already on them.
  • Ladle: For pouring liquid into the jar. Metal or silicone work, but I prefer the kind with a pour spout.

Nice-to-Haves:

  • Magnetic Lid Lifter: Tongs work just fine here, but I do love my lid lifter! It helps eliminate the steam and splash burns when working around boiling water.
  • Canning Lid Rack: This helps circulate the water all around the lids to ensure sterilization. I do not own one, but it's on my wishlist
  • Jelly Strainer: If you prefer jelly without seeds or pulp, this tool helps you achieve a smooth, traditional jelly texture.
  • Food Mill: Helps create smooth, seed-free, skin-free purées without needing to peel or seed everything by hand. Another wishlish item for me!

    • Luxury Items:

  • Digital Scale: It’s helpful for getting precise measurements, especially if you’re scaling recipes, measuring produce by weight, or following recipes that list ingredients in grams or ounces. But for most home canners, especially beginners, it’s not essential. Volume measurements (like cups and tablespoons) are standard in most tested recipes, and they work just fine. If you're following trusted sources and sticking to safe methods, a scale is a nice upgrade—but definitely not a requirement.
  • Pressure Canner: A pressure canner is essential if you’re preserving low-acid foods—but that doesn’t mean you need the most expensive model on the market. High-end pressure canners often come with features like extra-large capacity, built-in pressure gauges, premium materials (like all-stainless steel), or even electric automation. So while a top-tier pressure canner might make the process smoother or faster, it’s not a requirement. That’s why it lands in the luxury category: nice to have, but not necessary to get started.
  • Kilner Jars: Kilner jars are beautiful, well-made, and have a long history—but they’re also pricey compared to standard mason jars. They often come with clip-top lids, rubber seals, and a more “giftable” look, which makes them popular for presentation or short-term storage. But when it comes to safe, long-term canning, especially in the U.S., most tested recipes are designed for two-piece lid systems (like Ball or Kerr jars). Kilner jars can be used for canning, but they’re less common here and may require extra attention to sealing and processing methods. They’re great if you want something that looks nice on a shelf or for gifting, but they’re not necessary for safe, everyday canning. That’s why they fall into the luxury category—more about aesthetics than function.
  • Electric Automatic Water Bath Canner: Electric water bath canners are convenient, especially if you don’t want to tie up your stovetop or deal with monitoring boiling water. They often come with built-in timers, temperature controls, and spouts for easy draining. Some even double as slow cookers or beverage dispensers. But, you can do the exact same job with a large stockpot and a rack. The electric version is all about convenience, not necessity. It can make the process easier, especially if you’re canning frequently or in small spaces, but it’s not required to get started or to can safely.
  • Label Maker: A custom label printer can make your jars look polished and professional—great for gifting, selling, or just keeping your pantry extra organized. You can print labels with dates, ingredients, batch numbers, or even your own branding. But when it comes to function, it’s not essential. A sharpie and masking tape, or even printable labels from your home printer, will do the job just fine. What matters most is that your jars are clearly labeled with what’s inside and when it was canned.
  • Digital pH Meter: A digital pH meter can be a helpful tool if you’re developing your own canning recipes or working with borderline-acid foods. It gives you a precise reading of a food’s acidity level, which is important because safe water bath canning requires a pH of 4.6 or lower to prevent the growth of harmful bacteria like Clostridium botulinum. That said, if you’re using tested, trusted recipes from reliable sources (like USDA, Ball, or university extensions), you don’t need to test pH yourself. Those recipes have already been developed and verified for safety

    • Water Bath Canning

    This method is for high-acid foods. Think fruit jams, jellies, pickles, tomatoes (with added acid), and most fruit preserves. You submerge jars in boiling water (212°F) for a set amount of time. The heat kills off mold, yeast, and most bacteria, and the acidity prevents the growth of more dangerous organisms like Clostridium botulinum.

    • Good for: jams, jellies, fruit butters, pickles, salsa (with tested recipes)
    • Equipment needed: large pot with a rack, jars, lids

      • Pressure Canning

      This method is for low-acid foods, like egetables, meats, beans, broths, and anything that isn’t pickled or naturally acidic. These foods require higher temperatures (240°F+) to kill off harmful bacteria, especially botulism spores. A pressure canner traps steam to reach those higher temps safely.

      • Good for: green beans, corn, soups, stews, meats, legumes
      • Equipment needed: pressure canner (not the same as a pressure cooker)
